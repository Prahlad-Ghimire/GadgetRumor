Searching for visuals on the web is easier than ever.

By David Nield Published September 13, 2024 | Comments

On Desktop (PC/Mac):

1. Ensure Chrome is Updated: Make sure you are using the latest version of Chrome. Google Lens is integrated directly into the Chrome browser, so updating Chrome to the most recent version is important.

2. Open Chrome and Visit a Website:
Go to a website with an image you want to analyze or right-click on any image on the webpage.

3. Use Google Lens:
Right-click on the image you want to use with Google Lens. From the context menu that appears, select "Search image with Google Lens." A new panel will open on the right side of the browser, displaying results and information related to the image (such as similar images, text, or objects detected).

4. Explore Results:
Google Lens will identify objects, text, landmarks, and other elements in the image, providing links and additional information.

On Mobile (Android/iOS):
If you want to use Google Lens on a mobile device, you can do so via the Google Lens app or directly through the Google Chrome app (Android only):

1. Open the Chrome App:
Open the Chrome app on your phone and visit the webpage with the image.

2. Use Google Lens:
Tap and hold on the image you want to search with Google Lens. From the context menu, tap "Search with Google Lens." You’ll see similar results, information, and visual recognition related to the image. This integration works both on desktop and mobile versions of Chrome, but the desktop experience is more advanced for now, with the option to analyze images directly from web pages.

The best uses for Google Lens in Chrome



1. Identify Objects and Landmarks

Use Case: If you encounter an image of a famous landmark or object, Google Lens can identify it for you.
Example: See an image of a historical monument or a rare item on a webpage? Google Lens will provide information about it, including its name, history, or related websites.

2. Text Translation and Copying

Use Case:You can use Google Lens to extract and translate text from an image. This is particularly helpful when browsing non-English websites or when you come across text in images.
Example: Right-click on an image with foreign text, and Lens will allow you to translate the text into your preferred language.

3. Product Search and Shopping.

Use Case:Google Lens can be used to search for products within images, allowing you to find the exact product or similar ones available online.
Example: If you see a stylish dress or tech gadget on a webpage, right-click and use Google Lens to find where to buy it or get more details about the product.

4. Identify Plants and Animals

Use Case: If you find an image of a plant or an animal, Google Lens can provide insights into its species, care instructions, or other relevant details.
Example: Spot a plant you like on a gardening website? Use Google Lens to identify its name and care tips instantly.

5. Learn About Artwork

Use Case: Google Lens can identify artwork and provide information about the artist, era, and context.
Example: See a famous painting on a museum website? Right-click and Google Lens will tell you about the painting and its artist.

6. Scan Barcodes and QR Codes

Use Case: You can use Google Lens to scan barcodes or QR codes directly from images on websites.
Example: Scan a QR code in an image that links to a discount or special offer while shopping.

7. Visual Search for Similar Images

Use Case: Google Lens allows you to find similar images or objects across the web, helping you explore more content related to your.
Example: Discover more images or variations of a product or an aesthetic by using Google Lens to search visually.

8. Find Recipes and Ingredients

Use Case: Google Lens can recognize food items in images, allowing you to search for recipes and related cooking instructions.
Example: Spot a dish you want to try on a cooking blog? Right-click the image, and Google Lens can help you find the recipe and cooking methods.

9. Get Information on Books, Movies, or Music

Use Case: Lens can recognize book covers, movie posters, and album artwork, providing more details about the content.
Example: Curious about a book you've seen online? Right-click the cover image, and Google Lens can tell you the title, author, and related reviews.

10. Extract Text for Note-Taking

Use Case: Google Lens can extract text from images, which is perfect for digitizing notes or quotes from websites or PDFs.
Example: Need to copy a quote from an image or article but don’t want to type it manually? Use Google Lens to extract the text for easy copying.
